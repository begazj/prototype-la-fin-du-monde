The Maquettes

Two maquettes for La Fin du monde filmée par l’Ange N.-D are known. The term “maquette” in the case of a livre d’artiste (artist’s book) means a model for the book in part or whole, which may incorporate trial printings. One of the maquettes is in the collections of the Bibliothèque Nationale de France. It relates only to a small part of the book and does not incorporate trial printings. The other, which is much more complete and incorporates trial printings, belongs to The Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York; it is bound in with one of the exemplaires de tête (deluxe) copies, which is digitally presented here with the New York maquette.

The New York Maquette

The later of the two maquettes, which is now in the collection of The Met, is bound together in one volume with a copy of the deluxe exemplaire de tête edition. The deluxe edition was printed on high quality, handmade paper that ensures more intense black for the type and more saturated color for the images and colored inscriptions. The maquette and the deluxe copy of the book are in a tooled leather binding, which is a sculpted work in its own right. Signed by Georges Leroux and dated 1972, it is housed in a blue buckram case lined with yellow suede. The maquette is printed on the lower quality paper used for the 1,200 copies of the main edition. The paper for both the copy and the maquette, “in-4 raisin” (in-quarto), measures 12⅞ x 10⅝ inches (32.8 x 27 cm).

Whereas the Paris maquette is Cendrars’s, the New York maquette is Léger’s. It consists of twenty-nine leaves, or fifty-nine pages. All but one of the leaves are two pages folded together. There are eleven pages on which Léger has variously worked with watercolor, gouache[?], and graphite, interspersed with pages of text, as finally printed, intervening.

The maquette begins with the book’s title page, printed without color (fig. xx), followed by the title page of the first chapter, also printed in black. Léger has used it like a sketchbook page to try out two colored motifs: a plant motif, white on a blue background, and a motif sometimes referred to in French as a meduse (jellyfish), which is outlined in graphite and colored in salmon pink. He has sketched in the legs in various positions. These two motifs would appear with another motif, a star, in chapter 6 in the book as printed. They are the only images in the book that do not simply accompany text or prepare the reader for text, but over or under which text is printed, so that they catch the eye in the process of reading.

Immediately following is the opening double-page spread for chapter 1, printed in black, to which Léger has added pictorial elements in yellow and upper-case lettering, outlined in pencil and colored by hand in red above and yellow below (Image xx). The lettering below spells “CAISSE” (cash register), and is more quickly outlined and only partially filled in with yellow. Underneath, written in graphite, is a note giving chrome yellow as the exact color to be used. This is clearly a new idea and would be used in the book, where it directly challenges the reader to choose between focusing on Léger’s word intervention in bright yellow and the dense black type of Cendrars’s text. This is the only place in the maquette where Léger also outlined in graphite possible new pictorial elements. They are sketched across the fragment of the “N” and the “GLI” of the word “English” in red, and they echo the curve of the upper case “G.” Léger would take them no further.

The chapter 1 double spread is one of five images where pictorial elements, recognizable icons, and words combine to tell visually Cendrars’s story; the others are the double-page spreads for chapters 2 and 5, and the frontispieces for chapters 3 and 4. When making the maquette, Léger already had trial printings of these storytelling illustrations in black supplied by Frazier-Soye and was at the stage of deciding on the color to be added by Richard, the colorist. His gouache and watercolor[?] additions, made directly and impulsively by hand, are antecedents to the models with color from which, using pochoirs (stencils), the final images for the book would be completed. There are instances of minor changes in color choices between the maquette and the published version, and one occasion where the change is more important: the maquette leaf devoted to the double-page spread that introduces chapter 5 (Image xx), where both pink and yellow areas are brushed in. For the book as published, Léger would expand the areas of yellow and get rid of the pink altogether (Image xx).

One of the main claims for the book as a new departure for early twentieth-century livres d’artiste is made by the color images that are not storytelling. There are three instances in which lettering is used almost exclusively as a pictorial element in its own right and four where lettering or cyphers are used both to arrest attention visually and to convey information. In all the latter instances, before coloring them in, Léger carefully outlined the cyphers or upper-case letters in graphite and, to ensure consistency in height and proportions, he used a ruler to box them in. The first instance is the number code set out in white on a red band at the end of chapter 2 (Image xx), which is inserted after the text, as it would be in the book. The second is a series of letters colored gray on a narrow band of yellow that spill down the page spelling nothing (Image xx). The third spells out the words “SPRICHT DEUTSCH” (German spoken) white on a band of gray (Image xx). The fourth spells out the words “SI PARLA ITALIANO” (Italian spoken) printed by pochoir (stencil) in red (Image xx). These inscriptions are superimposed over Cendrars’s text, so that they attract the eye away from the text just as the yellow letters spelling “CAISSE” do in the opening double page spread for chapter 1. In the book as published, Léger would have them printed, not across pages of text, but across the title pages of chapters 6 and 7, where their potential for distraction would be much reduced (Images xx and xx).

The three compositions where letters are dispersed as pictorial elements and fill whole pages do not challenge the black type of the text directly, but in the book as published are given pages of their own. In the maquette only one of them has been tried out, the frontispiece of chapter 7 (Image xx). In the book as published, it would be on the left verso page. Here it is on the right, recto page. The image is very close to how it would be in the book as published: all the color choices have been made, and Léger is sure enough of the shaping of letters and pictorial elements to outline them with pencil, changing his mind only once, where at top right he lets the yellow spread outside the outlined form, anticipating that form’s enlargement in the book. The other two lettering compositions in the book as published are just blank pages in the maquette. The first, for chapter 4, was finally to be placed opposite the image of the Angel N.-D with trumpet to lips on the recto, the right page, but the blank here is on the verso, the left page (Images xx and xx). Where the second was to be in the book, on the verso page following the title page of chapter 6, is another blank (Image xx).

Opposite the provisional chapter 7 frontispiece, on the left page, is the only instance in the maquette where Léger is still early on in the process of conceiving a new illustration (Image xx). The upper case letters here are outlined heavily in graphite over more sketchy beginnings, but no color is added. Léger is testing whether the letters forming his “si parla Italiano” inscription can be dispersed across the page to make a self-sufficient composition. The letters spelling “SI PARLA” can be read arranged vertically, crossed by those spelling “ITALIANO” arranged horizontally, forming a kind of cross. He would decide not to take the idea any further.

The fact that in the New York maquette there are blank pages where in the book there would be lettering compositions at the opening of chapters 4 and 6, and that the only idea left unresolved in the maquette is for a lettering composition, tends to confirm the evidence of the Paris maquette: Léger decided to develop compositions that give letters a lead pictorial role only after he had almost finished composing the storytelling illustrations for chapters 1, 2, 3 and 5, and was working with color. Additionally, Léger only uses pencil outlining in the maquette when he is testing ideas, most often when inserting inscriptions and using lettering as a pictorial element. With the exception of the double-spread opening of chapter 1, all the trial ideas in the maquette that Léger is in the process of finalizing are for chapters 6 and 7, the last two chapters of the book. Altogether there is a strong argument for concluding that, when Léger moved on from the earlier chapters for which he had supplied storytelling illustrations and confronted the question of how to give a visual accompaniment to Cendrars’s narrative of the end of the world, he decided that inscriptions and colored lettering compositions that complemented but did not illustrate the text were what would work best.

Cendrars’s text, as typeset, continues through the maquette between the illustrations and the lettering compositions, much as it would in the book, with one especially noticeable exception. It involves chapters 3 and 4, where uncertainties about how specific passages relate to each other in Cendrars’s text may be betrayed, but where also Léger may have simply been casual when fitting the text together and made mistakes.

The text of chapter 6 gets as far as the first three lines of paragraph 35, leaving that paragraph on an unfinished sentence. The last fourteen pages of the book, including the whole of the text of chapter 7, are left out, although they probably were already either printed or ready to be printed.
